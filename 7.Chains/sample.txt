The French Revolution began in 1789 in France and became one of the most important political revolutions in world history. The revolution started because of widespread economic problems, high taxation, food shortages, and dissatisfaction with King Louis XVI and the monarchy. The French society was divided into three estates: the clergy, the nobility, and the common people. The common people, known as the Third Estate, faced heavy taxes while the upper classes enjoyed privileges. One major event of the revolution was the storming of the Bastille prison on July 14, 1789. This event became a symbol of resistance against royal authority. During the revolution, the National Assembly introduced several reforms, including the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, which promoted liberty, equality, and popular sovereignty. As the revolution progressed, political instability increased. Radical groups such as the Jacobins gained power. Maximilien Robespierre became one of the leading figures during the Reign of Terror, a period in which thousands of people were executed using the guillotine. Many individuals suspected of opposing the revolution were arrested and executed. Eventually, the revolution led to the rise of Napoleon Bonaparte. In 1799, Napoleon seized power through a coup d'état and later became Emperor of France. Although the revolution ended the absolute monarchy, it also caused years of violence and political instability. However, its ideas strongly influenced later democratic movements across Europe and the world.
